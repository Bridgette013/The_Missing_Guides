WHAT'S HAPPENING MEDICALLY

The Detox Process Explained

Alcohol detoxification is the process of the body eliminating alcohol and adjusting to functioning without it. For heavy, chronic drinkers, this process is dangerous without medical supervision because the body has become physically dependent on alcohol to regulate basic functions.

What the Hospital is Doing:

1. Preventing Dangerous Complications

Alcohol withdrawal can cause seizures, cardiac issues, and delirium tremens (DTs). The medications he's receiving (typically benzodiazepines like Ativan, Librium, or Valium) prevent these life-threatening complications.

2. Managing Symptoms

Medications reduce the severity of withdrawal symptoms‚Äîtremors, anxiety, nausea, sweating. The doses are adjusted based on regular CIWA assessments (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment scale).

3. Correcting Nutritional Deficiencies

Heavy drinking depletes essential vitamins. He's likely receiving thiamine (B1), folate, and other B vitamins through IV or oral supplements to prevent serious complications like Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome.

4. Monitoring Vital Signs

Blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, and oxygen levels are checked regularly. Withdrawal puts stress on the cardiovascular system.

5. Treating Co-occurring Conditions

Depression, anxiety, sleep disorders, liver damage, GI issues‚Äîall common in chronic alcohol use and all being assessed and addressed.

The goal of medical detox is safe stabilization‚Äîgetting him through withdrawal alive and as comfortable as possible. It is NOT addiction treatment. Detox addresses the physical dependence. Treatment addresses the addiction itself.

UNDERSTANDING THE DISCHARGE PROCESS

What Happens During Discharge Planning

Typically begins on Day 4-5 of hospital stay, once acute withdrawal has resolved and he's medically stable.

The Discharge Team Will:

  • Assess his readiness for discharge (medically stable, withdrawal managed)
  • Evaluate his risk level for relapse

  • Provide referrals to treatment facilities
  • Coordinate insurance authorization if needed
  • Prescribe discharge medications
  • Schedule follow-up appointments
  • Provide crisis resources and relapse prevention information
Discharge planning often happens QUICKLY‚Äîsometimes with only 24-48 hours notice. Don't wait. Start asking about discharge plans on Day 3 or 4, even if they say it's "too early." It's not.

UNDERSTANDING TREATMENT OPTIONS

After medical detox, there are three primary paths:

‚úì OPTION 1: RESIDENTIAL INPATIENT TREATMENT

MOST RECOMMENDED - Highest Success Rate

What It Is:

30-90 day residential program where he lives at the facility full-time. Intensive therapy, medical management, structured environment, 24/7 support.

Why It Works:

  • Removes ALL triggers and access to alcohol
  • Builds coping skills in controlled environment before returning to real world
  • Addresses underlying issues (trauma, mental health, relationship patterns)
  • Establishes routine and accountability
  • Peer support from others in recovery
  • Medical oversight for any complications or co-occurring conditions
  • Family therapy and discharge planning built in

Typical Structure:

  • Individual therapy (3-5 sessions/week)
  • Group therapy (daily)
  • 12-step or alternative recovery meetings
  • Life skills, relapse prevention education
  • Recreational therapy, mindfulness, exercise
  • Medication management if needed

Cost/Insurance:

Many programs accept insurance. Out-of-pocket costs vary widely ($5,000-$30,000+ for 30 days). Scholarships and payment plans often available.

OPTION 2: INTENSIVE OUTPATIENT PROGRAM (IOP)

MODERATE RECOMMENDATION - Requires Strong Support System

What It Is:

Lives at home, attends treatment 3-5 days/week for 3-4 hours/day. Typically 8-12 weeks. Group therapy, individual sessions, education.

When It Might Work:

  • First-time seeking treatment with no prior relapses
  • Strong, sober support system at home
  • High motivation and insight into addiction
  • No co-occurring severe mental health issues
  • Stable housing situation
  • Able to remove all alcohol from home environment
  • Employment or family obligations make inpatient impossible

The Challenges:

  • Returns to same environment where drinking occurred
  • All the same triggers, stressors, people, places
  • Limited accountability (honor system between sessions)
  • Higher relapse rate than residential
  • Requires immense self-discipline during vulnerable period

OPTION 3: HOME WITH MINIMAL SUPPORT

NOT RECOMMENDED - Highest Relapse Risk

What It Is:

Goes home after detox with outpatient therapy appointments (1x/week or less), maybe AA meetings, no structured program.

Why This Usually Fails:

  • Detox only addressed physical dependence, NOT addiction
  • No tools learned for coping without alcohol
  • Returns immediately to all triggers and stressors
  • Minimal accountability
  • Easy access to alcohol
  • No peer support system established
  • Underlying issues (mental health, trauma) unaddressed

The Reality of Relapse Rates:

Studies show that 40-60% of people relapse within the first year after detox alone. Many relapse within days or weeks without continuing treatment.

With intensive treatment (inpatient + aftercare), success rates improve significantly‚Äîsome studies show 70-80% maintaining sobriety after comprehensive treatment.

If This Is The Path He Chooses:

You need to know your boundaries NOW. Turn to Section 5 (Treatment Conversations) for how to handle this scenario.

Things to establish immediately:

  • No alcohol in the house
  • He attends AA/support group meetings daily
  • Random drug/alcohol testing
  • Outpatient therapy minimum 2x/week
  • If he drinks, he leaves the house

Do not enable. Do not make excuses. Do not pretend it's working if it's not.

HANDLING MANIPULATION TACTICS

He may not do this intentionally, but addiction comes with manipulation. Recognizing the tactics helps you stay firm.

TACTIC: Guilt Tripping

Sounds like: "You don't trust me." "You're treating me like a child." "After everything I've been through, you're going to abandon me now?"

YOUR RESPONSE:

"This isn't about trust. This is about your health. The medical professionals are recommending treatment. I'm supporting that recommendation. I love you, and that's why I'm asking you to do this."

TACTIC: Minimizing

Sounds like: "It wasn't that bad." "I've got it under control now." "Other people have it way worse than me."

YOUR RESPONSE:

"You were hospitalized for detox. That's how bad it was. 'Under control' would have been not ending up in the ER. You need treatment. This isn't up for debate."

TACTIC: Future Promising

Sounds like: "I promise I'll go to meetings." "I'll start therapy next week." "Just give me a chance to prove I can do this."

YOUR RESPONSE:

"I want to believe you. But promises aren't a treatment plan. You need a structured program with professional support. You can prove you're serious BY going to treatment, not instead of it."

TACTIC: Anger/Intimidation

Sounds like: "You can't tell me what to do!" "I'll just leave then!" "Fine, I'll go drink if that's what you think I'm going to do anyway!"

YOUR RESPONSE:

"You're right, I can't control what you do. But I can control what I accept in my life. If you choose not to get help, you're choosing the consequences that come with that. I hope you don't."

If he becomes aggressive or threatens violence, disengage immediately. You do not have to tolerate abuse. Call 988 (crisis line) or 911 if you feel unsafe.

HOSPITAL SURVIVAL GUIDE

Practical Tips for Getting Through Hospital Days

What to Bring to the Hospital

  • Phone charger - You'll be there longer than you think
  • Snacks - Hospital food is terrible and expensive
  • Water bottle - Hospitals are dehydrating
  • Sweater or jacket - It's always freezing
  • Headphones - For when you need to tune out
  • This binder - So you can reference sections as needed
  • Book or tablet - Waiting rooms are boring
  • Notebook and pen - For taking notes during doctor conversations

Managing Hospital Time

  • You don't have to stay all day. Seriously. It's okay to go home.
  • Visiting during peak withdrawal (Days 2-3) is HARD. He may not recognize you, may be confused or agitated. Short visits are fine.
  • Ask the nurses for the real updates. They know more than he does about how he's actually doing.
  • Take breaks. Walk outside. Sit in your car. Leave the hospital and get lunch somewhere else.
  • You don't owe anyone an explanation for leaving. "I need to take care of some things" is enough.

Daily Self-Check During Hospital Stay:

  • Did I eat today? (Not just hospital vending machine chips)
  • Did I drink water?
  • Did I get fresh air at some point?
  • Did I sleep at least 5 hours last night?
  • Did I talk to someone who's not a medical professional?
  • Did I do something that's not hospital-related today?

If you checked fewer than 4 boxes, you need to prioritize yourself more tomorrow.

EMOTIONAL SURVIVAL

What You Might Be Feeling (And Why That's Normal)

It's Normal to Feel:

Relief - He's safe. Someone else is in charge. You can breathe for a minute.

Exhaustion - You've been running on adrenaline. Now that the immediate crisis is over, you're crashing.

Anger - At him. At the situation. At yourself. At everything.

Guilt - For not catching it sooner. For being angry. For wanting to leave the hospital. For ALL of it.

Hope and Fear at the Same Time - Maybe this is the turning point. Maybe it's not. You don't know which to believe.

Numb - Sometimes you feel nothing at all because it's too much to process.

ALL OF THESE ARE NORMAL. You're not broken. You're human.

Give Yourself Permission To:

  • Not have all the answers
  • Say "I don't know" when people ask how you're doing
  • Cry in the hospital bathroom
  • Feel angry at him even though he's sick
  • Not want to be at the hospital every waking moment
  • Take a nap instead of visiting hours
  • Tell people you don't want to talk about it
  • Change your mind about your boundaries
  • Ask for help
  • Put yourself first sometimes
BOUNDARIES WITH YOURSELF

You've set boundaries with him. Now you need to set some with yourself.

Stop Doing This:

‚ùå Googling "alcoholism recovery rates" at 3am
It won't help. The statistics won't tell you what HE will do. Close the laptop.

‚ùå Replaying every conversation to find what you "should have said"
You said what you said. It's done. You can't change the past by obsessing over it.

‚ùå Blaming yourself for not "seeing the signs" sooner
Addiction is sneaky. It hides. Even trained professionals miss it sometimes. You're not a failure.

‚ùå Checking your phone every 5 minutes waiting for hospital updates
Put the phone down. They'll call if something changes. You checking doesn't make time move faster.

‚ùå Explaining or justifying his behavior to others
You don't owe anyone an explanation. "He's dealing with some health issues" is enough.

‚ùå Putting your life completely on hold
Yes, this is a crisis. No, you don't have to cancel everything else in your life indefinitely.

AL-ANON: SUPPORT FOR YOU

Al-Anon is a support group for families and friends of alcoholics. It's not about HIM. It's about YOU.

Why Al-Anon Matters

You've been living in his chaos. Al-Anon gives you a room full of people who understand that chaos because they've lived it too. You don't have to explain. You don't have to justify. You can just BE.

What you'll learn:

  • How to detach with love (care about him without being destroyed by his choices)
  • How to set boundaries and actually keep them
  • How to stop enabling without feeling like you're abandoning him
  • How to take care of yourself without guilt
  • How to recognize when you're trying to control things you can't control
  • How to grieve the person he was while dealing with the person he is

Most importantly: You'll realize you're not alone. You're not crazy. And you're not the only one who's been through this.
